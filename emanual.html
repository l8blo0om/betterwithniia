How to use this manual

This is a teaching document, not a marketing brochure. Every exercise is written so that opening to any page tells you what the body is doing, how to set it up, how it breathes, what you are actually teaching, and what tends to go wrong — in plain language first, with the official Gyrotonic name attached so the certification vocabulary stays intact.

Each exercise leads with everyday language describing what the body does, then names it. After the name is introduced once, that name is used going forward. The aim is that the plain language teaches and the Gyrotonic name files it.

Movement descriptions are drawn together from the photographs in the source manual, the Main Characteristics and Important Attention notes for each series, standard Gyrotonic methodology, and the handwritten notes already in the binder. This is a working draft meant to be checked against what you know from your training and refined as you teach from it — the value here is a consistent, scannable structure that the original lacks, with room built in for the cues that were living in the margins.



The template

Every exercise in this manual is run through the same fields, in the same order, so the document is predictable and scans fast:

Lineage. Where this exercise comes from — for example, the handle-unit version of a pole exercise, or a member of a Final Format family — so the learning version and the finished version are never held apart in your head. Used where it applies.

Setup. The starting position: where the body sits, what the hands hold, how the equipment is arranged.

The movement. What the body does, step by step, in plain language.

Breath. How the movement breathes. The working default follows the Gyrotonic principle — exhale as the body narrows, curls, or flexes; inhale as it opens, arches, or extends — unless the source or your notes specify something else, such as a double breath or a sniffing breath.

What you're teaching. The actual point of the exercise — the named characteristic it develops and the quality you are watching for.

Common errors & corrections. What tends to go wrong and the cue that fixes it.

Modifications. Adaptations for client limitations, and any A/B variation where the source prints only one option. (Added field — not in the original; fill in from your own teaching.)

Reps / tempo. How many cycles and at what pace. (Added field — not in the original; a placeholder for you to set.)



The system, in plain language

The Gyrotonic Expansion System trains the muscles while mobilizing and articulating the joints. Its distinguishing feature is that it stretches and strengthens at the same time and with little strain, building range of motion and coordination together rather than one at the expense of the other.

The equipment is built around the way the human body actually moves. It allows freedom through a full range and a full speed, supporting movement rather than fighting it. Where conventional machines tend to work in straight lines or isolate a single joint — which can build strength that does not coordinate well — this system works multiple joints at once through circular and spiraling patterns, which also conditions the surrounding ligaments and their attachments.

The movement patterns are meant to be smooth, continuous, and uninterrupted, bridging contraction and extension so there is no hard stop between them. The rotating patterns of the joints build balanced support around the skeleton. Movement is organized through spherical awareness — a sense of the body moving in all directions around its own center — and circularity, carried on continuous guidance and adjustable resistance. Because the resistance stays even through every change of direction and around the central axis, the work distributes evenly between a muscle and its opposite, lengthening one while the other contracts.

Each exercise is paired with a breathing pattern and a rhythm, so the work can be gentle or vigorous depending on speed and intensity. Particular attention goes to the spine: improving how freely and capably it moves, which reduces wear over time. The resistance has been described as feeling like moving through water — full, smooth, and adjustable. The method is used by dancers and athletes, in rehabilitation settings, and across a wide range of people from children to seniors.

Source: Gyrotonic Expansion System, Juliu Horvath. Gyrotonic International Headquarters, Dingmans Ferry, PA — www.gyrotonic.com. Retained for context; not intended for teaching from directly.


The two ladders — how this manual is organized

The Pulley Tower material runs on two progression systems stacked on top of each other, and keeping them apart is what makes the whole thing legible. Naming them up front so the rest of the manual reads cleanly:

Ladder one — building a client into Foundation. This is taught in five steps, written in the source as “Step 1 toward Progression 1” through “Step 5 toward Progression 1.” Each step revisits every series and layers one new thing onto it: Step 1 establishes the shapes, Step 2 adds the breathing pattern, Step 3 adds the engagement of the legs, Step 4 adds the detailed positions and order, and Step 5 adds the narrowing of the pelvis. When all five steps are in the body, the client has Foundation — which the progression grid calls Progression 1.

Ladder two — growing a client beyond Foundation. This is the progression grid: Progression 1 (Foundation) through Progression 7, plus the Creative Series. Here each step adds a named exercise into a column — a new arch-and-curl variation, a new hamstring, a new opening — while everything already learned repeats. This ladder assumes Foundation is already in place.

So the same word “Foundation” sits at the top of one ladder and the bottom of the other. Steps 1–5 grow someone up into it; Progressions 1–7 grow them up out of it. The manual below holds ladder one first (Foundation, taught step by step), then ladder two (the progression grid), then the homework sequence, then the exercise library that both ladders point into.




Foundation, taught step by step (Steps 1–5 toward Progression 1)

The five-step build of Foundation. Each step is taught with the same hands-on teaching method — the teacher shows and names the movement, then teacher and client move together, then the client moves alone while the teacher observes — and each step keeps everything from the steps before it. What changes step to step is the one new layer named at the top of each.



Step 1 — Establish the shapes

First contact. Establish the spinal-motion vocabulary on the pole and chairs, introduce the handle-unit Arch and Curl through one-arm articulation and Stirring, and lay the hamstring floor preparation.

Gyrokinesis Spinal Motions (pole, on chairs)

Position. Teacher and student sit on chairs at a 90-degree angle to each other.

The movement. In counts of 3. Four repetitions of each: arch into curl; spiral right into sideways arch left; sideways arch right into sideways arch left — holding each position about three seconds with the hands resting on top of the thighs. Finish with small waves forward, hands on the thighs. Repeat the whole sequence two to three times.

Attention. Always initiate each movement from the elevation of the spine — the “And…” before the move. Keep it slow enough that the student can follow, which is what the counts of 3 are for. Sideways arch: the palms support the opposite side of the head, the throat soft and in, fingertips hooked into the “dimple” behind the ear. Wave: walk the legs a little forward and open the angle of the knees; long, deep exhalation.

Hands on — the four cycles

  • Cycle 1: teacher shows all the movements and names them.

  • Cycle 2: teacher and client move together.

  • Cycle 3: together again, naming it as the homework.

  • Cycle 4: client moves alone, teacher observes. Focus on rhythm and fluidity.

Breath. No special attention at this stage; eventually a double breath, with a cleansing breath on the wave.

Notes. Homework for the client — the spinal-motions sequence.



Arch and Curl Series — preparation with the handle unit

Position. Straddle, facing the handle unit.

The movement. Step 1: articulate one arm at a time through a half circle — center, inward, center, outward — the spine following with a spiraling quality. Step 2: one arm at a time through a full circle, introverted and extroverted, the spine following. The same arm articulation is also done forward and back.

Attention. Stay in the student’s comfort zone; keep the movement continuous; keep the attention on the spiraling of the arm and always look toward the reaching, spiraling arm. In the center position, elevate the spine; with the arm extended, look over the hand and let the spine reach slightly forward; the bent elbows reach sideways.

Hands on. Step 1: teacher shows all the exercises (full arch and curl, forward and reverse) with simplicity, refers back to the wave forward on the chair, and explains the names. Step 2: student follows while the teacher applies hands on, standing alongside — one hand on top of the shoulder as the friendly and diagnostic hand, the other supporting the spiraling articulation of the arm: hooking around the crease of the elbow on the open-arm position, then brushing from the top of the elbow out to the hand to give extra reach at the end. Step 3: student repeats each movement without hands on; teacher observes; hold each position at least three seconds.

Breath. Double breath, exhaling with each extension.



Arch and Curl Series — Stirring

Position. Straddle, facing the handle unit.

The movement. Circle the arms around with a transition through the high-V position of the handles; free and fluid.

Attention. Look up — slight arch; look down — slight curl; a yawning sensation through the spine.

Hands on. Teacher stands in front. Support the upright position (ask to elevate the spine); support the arch in high V (ask to reach from the sternum); support the curl (slight resistance to the handles, explain the rolling-back of the spine); support the elevation (slight pull to open the elbows). Show the handle positions — elevation, spiral the arms, arch (high V), curl (low V) — and teach the transition positions. Then the student repeats.

Breath. No special attention; eventually an ocean / cleansing breath.



Hamstrings Series — preparation on the floor

Position. Sitting on the floor.

The movement. Step 1: explain the concept of the 5th line. Step 2: a scooping grip to arrange the flexed-foot position. Step 3: arrange and correct the alignment of the whole leg; check the strength point in flex and in point; transition from flex to point by grabbing the teacher’s fingers with the toes, moving up and over into the point. Then the kneading and shivering work: Step 4 — kneading one leg at a time, the heel pressing on the floor, keep the alignment, then add the kneading of the legs. Step 5 — kneading with alternating legs; learn the rocking movement of the upper body in the bowl-curl position, then add the kneading. Step 6 — learn the shivering point; let the student rest on the forearms. Step 7 — shivering with both legs for 20 seconds, then kneading with alternating legs for 20 seconds (energetic awakening).

Attention. Alignment of the legs — create space in the ankle, knee, and hip joint; keep the position of the flexed foot and move through the 5th line; full muscular contraction; keep the neck long.

Hands on. For the kneading, kneel behind the student and alternate a cradle grip of the ribcage, guiding the rocking movement of the body, head and shoulder dimple down. Kneeling sideways, cradle the ribcage and elongate the spine; alternate by supporting the legs — touch the strength point of the heels and ask to reach. Support the kneading by using a towel for the cradle position.

Breath. Kneading: exhale with each extension of the leg; eventually a sniffing breath.

Notes. Homework for the client — shivering with both legs for 20 seconds, kneading with alternating legs for 20 seconds, alternating for two minutes.

The hamstring exercises themselves — double leg pump, single leg pump, dolphin forward, single leg bicycle forward — are introduced here lying on the bench facing away from the tower, upper straps on the feet, and are detailed in the Exercise Library.




Step 2 — Add the breathing pattern

Everything from Step 1 continues. Step 2 lays the corresponding breathing pattern onto the spinal motions, introduces the Upper Body turtle combination, and brings in Unwinding.

Gyrokinesis Spinal Motions — add the breathing pattern

The movement. Same as Step 1, in counts of 3.

Breath. Inhale through the nose with the elevation of the spine and toward the center position, restraining the nostrils while inhaling. Exhale through the mouth while moving into the spinal position — arch and curl on a HO sound, toward a HO sound everywhere else. Wave forward: a long, expelling, cleansing exhalation on a HO sound.

Attention. Synchronize movement and breath — exhale and move continuously, inhale and move toward the center position. A short pause between inhalation and exhalation; a sound of satisfaction.

Hands on. Set 1: teacher performs the movement with the client, breathing together, slowing down to focus on the breath. Set 2: teacher and student move in rhythm with the breathing pattern. Set 3: student moves, teacher observes.

Notes. Homework — the spinal-motions sequence.



Arch and Curl Series — preparation with the handle unit

The movement. Same as Step 1, now with the breathing corresponding to the movement.

Hands on. Same as Step 1; breathe together with the client.



Upper Body Series — Turtle combination (dissected in 4 steps)

Position. Sitting on the bench in straddle, facing the tower, upper straps in the hands.

The movement. The full movement is a bow curl reaching forward toward the pulley tower, in four phases: reach — narrow the pelvis — turtle (curl) — elongate the cervical spine, shoulders down, reach down through the palms. Taught in four steps: Step 1, teacher shows and explains the four phases sitting on a chair; Step 2, teacher moves with the student, verbalizing, on a chair; Step 3, massage the area around cervical 7, hands on with the student on a chair; Step 4, teacher shows the movement with the cables.

Attention. All four exact positions dissected — reach, narrow, squeeze, elongate.

Hands on. Place the palms at the final position for the elbows and ask the student to touch them; in the final position support the extra squeeze of the elbows; support the elongation of the cervical spine by hooking up to the “dimple” behind the ear; support the shoulders down with the palms on top of the shoulders; support the reach through the 5th line of the palm.

Breath. One exhalation with each position.



Unwinding — Weight shift side to side

Position. Standing with the legs slightly opened.

The movement. Vacuum — suction of the feet, equal weight distribution, shifting the weight through the 5th line from side to side.

Attention. Demonstrate the suction / vacuum of the palms of the hands as an example for the feeling in the soles of the feet; little toe scooping up; the image of standing “without bones”; strengthen the leg underneath — do not extend it right away, but more and more.

Breath. With each step, continuous.

Notes. Give as homework to the client.

Twirling prep — add a slow, small turn with change of directions

The movement. Slow turns, legs close together.

Attention. The importance of the movement of the supporting leg; maintain all aspects learned in the weight-shift movement.

Breath. Exhale with each step.




Step 3 — Add the engagement of the legs

Everything from Steps 1 and 2 continues. Step 3 adds the engagement of the legs to the spinal motions and the Arch and Curl, and brings in the Legwork and Abdominal series.

Gyrokinesis Spinal Motions — add the engagement of the legs

The movement. Cycle 1: explain and add the engagement of the legs — arch, open the legs; curl, legs parallel, pivot on the heel; spiraling twist, opposition of the legs; sideways arch, open the legs, pivot on the heel, weight on both sit bones; wave forward, arching open the legs, curling back parallel (close). Repeat each movement at least four times. Cycle 2: details on the breathing — arch exhale on HA, curl exhale on HO; sideways arch a sound between ho and ha, more toward HO; wave forward HO, long and deep. Cycle 3: add the new exercises (learn just the new exercises).

Attention. For arch-curl, sideways arch, and spiral, introduce some details — for example a balanced arch and a balanced curl. Spiral with the curl over: the thigh bone stays long, the neck stays long, transition to the other side through a MODERATE arch position, shoulders staying down, exhale on HO. Side shift: the shoulder axis stays parallel to the floor, the “solar plexus” shifting parallel to the floor, a MODERATE shift of the ribcage, both sit bones staying on the chair while the weight shifts, the head staying slightly toward center.

Hands on. Perform hands on without interrupting the movement and flow; add hands on to support the narrowing of the pelvis. For the reverse wave: support the curl with the ribcage scoop grip, support the elevation by guiding the head into upright. To check the curl: “shoulder dimples down.”

New this step. Arch and Curl with engagement of the legs; wave reverse; plus the Legwork and Abdominal series below.

Breath. Inhale nose, exhale mouth as in Step 2; double breath; a cleansing breath on the wave.

Notes. Homework — the spinal motions including the new exercises.



Legwork Series — Seated Leg Work (new)

Position. Sitting on a chair; then with the upper straps on the feet, sitting on the bench facing the tower.

The movement. Leg lifts with leaning back, the legs off the floor, a long bow-curl position of the spine. Circles in both directions — beginner with a slight curl in the lower back, the feet slightly off the floor if possible, pointed; progressing to extended legs with flexed feet, then bent legs. Leg lifts into extension; circles both directions.

Attention. Leg lifts: sit at the edge of the bench and engage the hamstrings so the thighs lift slightly off the bench top. Circles: the feet stay flat over the floor, the legs parallel or naturally turned out; alignment of the foot-knee-thighbone relation; the knee guides the movement, not the feet; sit a bit higher and move from the bow-curl position toward a straighter spine. One leg reaches forward while one leg pulls underneath the bench, knees next to each other; the spine in a long bow curl is strong, balanced, connected; look up toward the pulley tower; the rhythm continuous, not too slow, playful; the feet stay pointed.



Abdominal Series — introduced

The movement. Curl ups; curl ups into arch with the legs bent; chest-pelvis lift. The Foundation curl-up material, brought to full form in Step 5.




Step 4 — Add the detailed positions and order

Everything continues. Step 4 introduces the specific order of the spinal-motion exercises and their detailed vocabulary, and dissects the handle-unit Arch and Curl into four steps.

Gyrokinesis Spinal Motions — add the details and the order

The movement. Introduce the specific order of the spinal-motion exercises and the vocabulary: balanced arch, balanced curl, even spiraling twist, balanced sideways arch, balanced curl in the cross-over position.

Hands on — the detailed positions

In the Foundation course only, use the drawings of the spinal motions from the manual for the detail of each movement. Where possible, rest the pole across the top of the spine at the spine of the scapulae for better feedback.

  • Balanced arch position: head and thoracic scoop. Use the movement of the wrist, no fingers in the face; use the forearms to guide the scapulae and thoracic spine; move with the direction of the scoop. (A pencil and a weight plate help the teacher learn the wrist-and-forearm movement.)

  • Balanced curl, ribcage scoop: direction diagonally backwards and upwards, creating space between the thoracic and lumbar spine; palming or fingertips on the last two ribs, not the abdomen.

  • Balanced curl, ilium scoop: direction diagonally backwards and upwards, creating space between the lumbar spine and the sacrum; palming or fingertips around the iliac spine.

  • Transition arch to curl: always guide the head into the elevated position; order — elevate, arch, elevate, curl.

  • Spiral: support the elongation in the maximum spiraling position; tap the top of the head and ask to elongate the spine.

  • Sideways shift: support the shift of the ribcage with direction — shift sideways, one hand lateral on the ribcage with attention to the position of the head, one hand lateral on the neck with direction.



Arch and Curl Series — introverted, dissected in 4 steps (new)

The movement. Four steps. Start in the high-V position of the handles and the arch of the spine; curl back (1) into the upright spine position (2); spiral the arms and initiate the arch (3); move into the high-V position of the handles (4). Four clear positions of the handles corresponding to four positions of the spine; find the position of the arms, body, and legs for the individual range of motion.

Hands on. Step 1: teacher shows the movement in four steps and introduces the handle positions with their corresponding spine positions. Step 2: student moves while the teacher applies hands on, synchronizing the movement of the legs. Set 1 standard; Set 2 hands on for the spine; Set 3 hands on for the legs.




Step 5 — Add the narrowing of the pelvis

The central principle, applied across all the exercises. When this is in the body alongside Steps 1–4, the client has Foundation.

Basic principle. Narrowing of the pelvis; stabilization through contrast; internal symmetry; the seed center.

Gyrokinesis Spinal Motions — the seated narrowing sequence

Teaching. Step 1: explain the basic idea of the narrowing of the pelvis — stabilization through contrast, stabilization through the seed center. Step 2: apply hands on to create the skeletal uprightness of the spine. Step 3: the movement sequence (2010 revision) below.

Preparation

  • Internal Figure 8s, sitting upright without too much muscular contraction: Part 1 — both palms on the solar plexus, weight shift forward and back in counts of 4; Part 2 — weight shift side to side in counts of 4; Part 3 — palms on top of the thighs or next to the body, figure 8s along the center line / 5th line; image: a figure 8 around the sit bones, then a figure 8 around the eyeballs.

  • Shoulder lift series: lift the shoulders all the way up and shorten the neck — hold — slowly bring the shoulders down and elongate the back of the neck — press the shoulders down and move the head side to side — release — repeat; eventually refine it into a disappearing-and-appearing action.

Movements

  • Movement 1, counts of 3: elevation of the spine + narrow the pelvis + squeeze the inner thighs — release.

  • Movement 2, counts of 3: narrow + elevate — spiraling twist to one side — back to center — release — repeat to the other side.

  • Movement 3, counts of 2: the same.

  • Movement 4, counts of 2: from regular sitting in center, narrow + move directly into the spiraling twist to one side — back to center — release — repeat to the other side.

  • Movement 5, one count each: narrow + elevate — spiraling twist one side — spiraling twist the other side — back to center — release.

  • Movement 6, arch-curl, one count each: narrow + elevate — arch — elevate — curl — elevate in the center — release.

  • Movement 7, wave continuous: narrow + elevate — wave forward — curling back — elevate — repeat. Use the hands to touch the medial side of the legs, the lateral side, and the top of the legs.

Attention. Observe the elevation and skeletal uprightness of the spine as one effect of the narrowing.



The narrowing, systematic hands-on

The heart of Step 5: a fixed order of hands-on cues that build the narrowing in the body.

Intention. Decompression of the lumbar spine, creating space between the vertebrae; elevation of the entire spine into uprightness, including the positioning of the pelvis; placing the legs “underneath the body.”

Images

  • The body is like a toothpaste tube open at both ends: squeeze the tube in the middle and the paste spreads out of both ends, up and down.

  • A metal belt around the line of the iliac spine.

  • A magnet in an egg shape on top of the bladder — the seed center, above the greater trochanter: spread the energy upwards to elevate the spine, and downwards to create space in the hip joint and ground the legs and feet.

Systematic hands on, in order

  • Elevate the head and elongate the cervical spine: two fingers on the soft part around the processus mastoideus, rather than the edge of the palm — the anticipation of the narrowing.

  • Shoulder dimple down: in the soft dimple of the acromio-clavicular joint, the soft point in the triangle of clavicle, acromion, and spine of the scapulae; if needed use three fingertips.

  • Open the shoulders to the side and open the chest: emphasize the opening laterally; a wrapping grip from inside or the top of the humerus head, the thumb on the scapulae, directed toward the ribs on a diagonal.

  • Synchronize the elevation of the head and the shoulders down with the connection of the ribcage diagonally toward the pubic bone; ask verbally for the quality and guide the ribcage.

  • Arrange the legs and the angle of the knees and feet: (a) reach out with the thigh bone — the teacher tractions the thigh bone and keeps the pelvis in place; (b) the student squeezes the inner thighs — support with one palm lateral at the femur head and one hand inside the thigh bone at the knee, helping the femur move “underneath the body”; (c) the student supports the squeeze of the inner thighs while the teacher places the palms lateral on the thighs near the knees to encourage the opening.



Abdominal Series — full form (new)

Position. Preparation on the floor; lying on the back on the floor or on the bench.

Exercises. Squeezing breath; simple cradle; large cradle; taming of the tiger. Added: standing on all fours, and standing on all fours upside down.

The movement

  • Squeezing breath: learn it sitting on a chair — the sound “hoh-h” (a double-h breath) from loud to silent, or a coughing image; exhale all the way; suspend, then exhale three more times, squeezing the golden egg, squeezing the balloon from all sides. The simple cradle position helps find it: create a contraction above the pubic bone.

  • Simple cradle: legs parallel and bent, ankles squeezed together, knees slightly apart, inner thighs squeezing; bowl-curl spine resting on the palms; roll down all the way to place the lumbar spine and sacrum, feet off the floor, with the squeezing breath; roll up, feet on the floor, squeezing breath; eventually in counts of 3.

  • Large cradle: legs and spine as before; roll down all the way to the kidney area, supported by the forearms, synchronized with the squeezing-breath exhalation; roll up, the legs staying off the floor, squeezing breath; eventually in counts of 3.

  • Taming of the tiger: legs and arms soft, as if hanging in the air; the angle between pelvis and legs bigger than 90 degrees; head up, looking between the knees, slightly diagonally up; fast breathing through the nose; finish with a deep exhalation through the mouth, slowly releasing — in the release it feels like fainting, releasing the seed center while keeping psoas control of the legs and arms; keep the area between the lumbar spine and the sacrum connected and on the floor.

Attention. Find the weak spot between Lumbar 5 and the sacrum. If possible, wrap a pole behind the occiput with the arms around it to ensure the elongation of the cervical spine and the decompression of the entire spine. For standing on all fours upside down — Grip 1: support the narrowing of the pelvis for the reaching of the legs; Grip 2: support the reaching of the arms. The work strengthens the weak spot between L5 and the sacrum, activates the biological pump of the pelvic diaphragm, elongates and decompresses the back of the body, engages the abdominals, and gives cranio-sacral stimulation.



Upper Body Openings — introduced (new)

Exercise. Shoulder mobilization in the frontal plane and the sagittal plane; overhead circles with the arms. Position: sitting on a chair.

The movement. Shoulder mobilization frontal — the phases of the upper arm: shoulders inward and up, spread the elbows to the side (the scoop), elbows stay up and shoulders move down. Sagittal — a starting position with the arms more in front, continuous, a massaging feeling. Overhead circles — palms facing the ceiling, hands close to the top of the head, the upper arm moving in-up-out-scoop and down, the whole arm synchronized; an even circle front-side-back-side.

Attention. Frontal plane: move the head of the humerus in the shoulder joint. Sagittal plane: massage the joint. Overhead circles: massage the joint while spreading the elbows.

Hands on. Frontal plane: sitting behind the student, guiding the upper arm. Sagittal plane: massaging the joint, guiding the shoulders, both palms on top. Overhead circles: standing behind the student, guiding the arms, or palms on top of the shoulders to keep them down with the fingers spread to keep the distance between the neck and the upper arm.

Breath. Inhale nose, exhale mouth.

Notes. Homework — the exercises sitting on a chair.



Unwinding — full form

The movement. Turning slowly with a change of direction; look straight forward, gazing into space.

Breath. Exhale with each step.

Notes. Homework for the client.

Gyrotonic Final Homework Sequence

The homework sequence reinforces the principle of the narrowing of the pelvis. Hands are introduced to support the skeletal uprightness of the spine without any compression.

Ingredients for the narrowing of the pelvis: head reaching up + shoulders down + shoulders open to the side + connect the ribcage + squeeze and lift at the narrowing area, squeeze and reach down with the legs + simultaneously reach forward with the knees + squeeze the inner thighs + reach outward with the knees + spread the soles of the feet / suction foot.

Part 1 — Seated on a stool

Preparation

  • Alternating shoulder lift-release; advanced version: a quality of appearing and disappearing.

  • Internal Figure 8s, arms next to the body (rather than palms resting on the thighs): figure 8s in one direction at the level of the sit bones, the seed center, the solar plexus, the sternum, the throat center, the eyeballs, and slightly above the top of the head — then reverse and start from the top of the head down.

  • The order of these two exercises can be interchanged.

Spinal motions

  • Elevation of the spine with narrowing of the pelvis, in counts of 3; palms supporting the intention: palms inside the thighs, palms outside the knees, arms reaching down next to the body.

  • Elevation of the spine with narrowing of the pelvis, in counts of 2, arms next to the body.

  • Spiraling to one side at a time, in counts of 2: center — spiral — center — slow release.

  • Spiraling side to side continuous: center — right — left — center — slow release; center — left — right — center — slow release. (Her note: Double Spiral.)

  • Spiraling directly from center to the side, one spiral at a time, in counts of 2: spiraling up — slowly return to center; use the hands to indicate the opposition of the leg. (Her note: Staircase.)

  • The last two are interchangeable.

  • Arch-curl continuous: narrow/elevate — arch — center/elevate — curl — center/elevate — slowly release.

Stimulation of the biological pump, seated

  • From slow to faster, a small movement of the pelvis; the accent is at the end of the movement.

  • Waves forward, from small to bigger: up to the umbilicus, up to the solar plexus, up to the chest, all the way to the head.

Part 2 — Floor work

  • Small cradle, 4 times. Attention: look diagonally up.

  • Large cradle, 4 times. Attention: look diagonally up.

  • Shivering and kneading of the legs. Attention: keep the neck long. Shiver for a minimum of one minute, sounding like raindrops; repeat 2 times.

  • Large cradle with knee-to-forehead combination: parallel — open into diamond — extend to the front, 4 times; reverse: diamond — legs parallel — extend to the front, 4 times. Attention: keep the neck long. (Prep: a folded chair under the sacrum.)

  • Hip-knee joint mobilization: 4 times forward, 4 times reverse.

  • Repeat the knee-to-forehead combination and the hip-knee joint mobilization.

  • Standing on all fours. Eventually this preparation is replaced by standing on all fours upside down.

  • On the floor: standing on all fours upside down. Attention: balanced effort from the legs and arms, initiating from the seed center; if possible the sacrum comes off the floor.

  • Directly into Taming of the Tiger. Attention: sit bones reaching forward, look toward the ceiling, slow release.

Series 1 — Arch and Curl

A spine series. It wakes the spine up segment by segment and moves it through its full forward-and-back range, releases the shoulders and arms, begins gentle stimulation for the heart and lungs, and brings the nervous system online. Across the series you are watching for synchronized movement and breath, uninterrupted flow, clear intention in the execution, a long pelvis and fully extended spine, and overall spherical awareness.

The series is taught in two stages. The Pole Exercises are the learning versions, done seated at the bench holding the pole with the straps, where the shape can be felt slowly and clearly. The Final Format versions are the grown-up versions of those same shapes on the handle unit, faced toward or away from the tower. Each Final Format exercise is the refined descendant of a pole exercise, so they are presented together.



Pole Exercises (learning versions)

Shared setup. Sit straddling the bench facing the tower, with the straps around the pole — upper or lower depending on the exercise — and the pole carried behind the midback or across the edge of the shoulder blades. The pole stays active throughout: pressed in toward the body and slightly up, never resting against the body or hanging on the cables.



Final Format (handle-unit versions)

Shared setup. On the handle unit. The first group is done straddling and facing the handle unit; a later group is done straddling and facing away from it. Each exercise below names its facing. The series finishes with a symmetrical exercise.

Series 2 — Hamstring

A leg series done on the handle unit, with the feet in the cables and the back supported, working the hamstrings and the whole leg line through circular and reaching patterns. On the session-flow page this is one of the series a class draws an exercise from, alongside the spine work, the abdominal series, the upper body openings, and the unwinding.

The source prints these as Final Format almost entirely as names and photographs, with little or no position text — so the descriptions below lean on the photographs, standard Gyrotonic leg-series methodology, and your notes. They want checking against your training. The directional grammar from the spine series repeats here: forward, reverse, flex, introverted, sideways, pelvis-flat. Each named exercise carries its orientation variants as states of one entry rather than as separate exercises.




Series 3 — Upper Body

The upper-body series of the Final Format, faced toward the tower with the upper straps in the hands. It opens the shoulder girdle and chest, builds reach and rotation through the arms and upper back, and carries the same spine principles into standing-arm work. Across the series you are watching for the shoulders staying down and open, the reach traveling through the 5th line of the palm, and the spine elevating rather than collapsing into the pull.



Twist and Pull Leaning Back — Twist and Pull, leaning back (Final Format)

Lineage. The Upper Body member of the spine family — the standing-arm cousin of the spiraling work introduced in the Arch and Curl series.

Setup. Sitting on the bench in straddle, facing the tower, upper straps in the hands.

The movement. Lean the torso back as a long unit while the arms pull, then add a twist: rotate the ribcage over a stable pelvis as one arm reaches and the other draws back, the spine staying long through the lean. Return through center and repeat to the other side.

Breath. Exhale into the twist and the pull; inhale returning through center.

What you're teaching. Rotation of the upper back over a stable base, with the shoulders staying down as the arms work; the lean comes from a long spine, not a collapse at the low back.

Common errors & corrections.

  • Shrugging the shoulders up into the pull — cue the shoulder dimple down.

  • Leaning from the low back instead of a long spine — cue reach from the crown, lean as one piece.

  • Letting the pelvis rotate with the twist — keep both sit bones anchored.

Modifications. Smaller lean and smaller twist first; build the range once the spine stays long. (Confirm against your training.)

Reps / tempo. Placeholder — set your standard.



Harvesting — Harvesting (Final Format)

Lineage. Upper Body series; the forward-and-reverse reaching pattern that gathers the arms in toward the body and opens them back out, like drawing a harvest in.

Setup. Sitting on the bench in straddle, facing the tower, upper straps in the hands.

The movement. Reach the arms forward and out, then draw them in toward the body in a gathering arc, the upper back rounding gently with the gather and opening with the reach. Forward version and reverse version.

Breath. Exhale gathering in; inhale reaching out and open.

What you're teaching. Coordinated opening and closing of the shoulder girdle with the breath, the chest opening on the reach and the back widening on the gather.

Common errors & corrections.

  • Pulling only with the arms — cue the movement to begin from the back and ribcage.

  • Losing the spine elevation on the gather — keep length even as the back widens.

Modifications. Reduce the range of the reach; keep the gather small until the shoulder stays settled. (Confirm against your training.)

Reps / tempo. Placeholder — set your standard.



Shoulder Release — Shoulder Release (Final Format)

Lineage. Upper Body series; the releasing counterpart to the openings work, mobilizing the shoulder joint through its circle.

Setup. Sitting on the bench in straddle, facing the tower, upper straps in the hands.

The movement. Move the head of the upper arm through the shoulder joint in a circle — forward, up, out, and around — releasing the joint while the spine stays elevated and the shoulders stay set away from the ears.

Breath. A continuous, easy breath through the circle; exhale on the downward, gathering part of the arc.

What you're teaching. Mobility and release in the shoulder joint itself, the arm circling freely while the torso stays quiet and tall.

Common errors & corrections.

  • The ribcage following the arm — keep the torso still and let the joint do the work.

  • The shoulder riding up at the top of the circle — keep the dimple down throughout.

Modifications. Smaller circles first, then widen; one arm at a time before both. (Confirm against your training.)

Reps / tempo. Placeholder — set your standard.
